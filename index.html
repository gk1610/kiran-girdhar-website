About

I am an Assistant Professor with joint appointments in the Department of Psychiatry and the Department of Genetics and Genomic Sciences at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai. I am also a member of the Center for Disease Neurogenomics, Friedman Brain Institute, and Icahn Institute for Data Science and Genomic Technology.

My research focuses on understanding how non-coding genetic variants contribute to neuropsychiatric disease risk by mapping cell-type-specific chromatin architecture in the human brain. Through population-scale epigenomic profiling of postmortem brain tissue, my work has revealed that chromatin accessibility patterns in adult schizophrenia brains mirror those found in the developing fetal brain, providing a molecular bridge between early neurodevelopmental processes and adult disease states.

A key finding from my research demonstrates that up to 90% of genetic risk sequences for schizophrenia and related psychiatric illness are located in the regulatory non-coding genome. By mapping cis-regulatory domains and their integration with three-dimensional chromatin topology, my work provides mechanistic insights into how psychiatric genetic risk is organized at the chromosomal level.

Research Focus

Chromatin Accessibility Profiling

Population-scale (N>1,300) chromatin accessibility profiling of neurons and non-neurons from neocortical brain regions including anterior cingulate cortex and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex.

3D Genome Organization

Mapping cis-regulatory domains (CRDs) that reveal fine-grained chromosomal organization integrated into Hi-C topologically associating domain stratification by nuclear topography.

Neurodevelopmental Origins of Psychiatric Disease

Investigating the link between fetal brain chromatin signatures and adult schizophrenia, revealing how upregulated chromatin changes in disease correlate with fetal cortical development.

Histone Modification Landscapes

Constructing promoter- and enhancer-enriched nucleosomal histone modification landscapes (H3K27ac, H3K4me3) to identify large-scale regulatory dysregulation in psychiatric disorders.

First Author Publications

The neuronal chromatin landscape in brains from individuals with schizophrenia is linked to early fetal development

Girdhar K, Bendl J, Baumgartner A, Therrien K, et al.

Nature Neuroscience (2025) 28:2451–2460

Population-scale chromatin accessibility profiling (N=1,393) of neurons and non-neurons from two neocortical regions reveals that upregulated open chromatin regions in schizophrenia neurons are associated with SCZ risk loci and strongly correlate with fetal brain chromatin signatures. Identified a trans-regulatory domain enriched for immature glutamatergic neurons that distinguishes individuals with schizophrenia in an independent cohort of >195,000 individuals from the Million Veteran Program.

Chromatin domain alterations linked to 3D genome organization in a large cohort of schizophrenia and bipolar disorder brains

Girdhar K, Hoffman GE, Bendl J, et al.

Nature Neuroscience (2022) 25:474–483

Constructed promoter- and enhancer-enriched nucleosomal histone modification landscapes (H3K27ac and H3K4me3) from 739 adult prefrontal cortex samples (388 controls, 351 SCZ/BD). Mapped thousands of cis-regulatory domains revealing fine-grained 10⁴–10⁶-bp chromosomal organization integrated into Hi-C TAD stratification. Large clusters of hyper-acetylated CRDs enriched for SCZ heritability with prominent representation of fetal development and glutamatergic signaling sequences.

Cell-specific histone modification maps in the human frontal lobe link schizophrenia risk to the neuronal epigenome

Girdhar K, et al.

Nature Neuroscience (2018) 21:1126–1136

Generated cell-type-specific histone modification maps from human frontal lobe tissue, linking schizophrenia genetic risk to neuronal epigenomic signatures.
